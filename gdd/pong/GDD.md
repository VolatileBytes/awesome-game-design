# Pong

Atari's 1972 arcade smash that launched the commercial video game industry. Two players each control a vertical paddle on one side of the screen. A ball bounces between them; missing the ball scores a point for the opponent. First to 11 wins.

Designed by Allan Alcorn as a training exercise for Nolan Bushnell (Atari co-founder), Pong's original hardware was built from discrete TTL logic chips — no CPU, no software, no framebuffer. Circuits directly generate the video signal and gameplay state. The training exercise shipped, and within a year Pong cabinets were everywhere. It codified the language of a generation of video games: paddles, ball physics, score in the corner, two players competing on a shared screen.

## Design Pillars

1. **One Page of Rules** — anyone understands Pong in 5 seconds. No tutorial.
2. **Pure Skill vs. Skill** — no randomness; ball physics deterministic.
3. **Physicality on Screen** — paddle responsiveness is the whole game feel.
4. **Escalating Tempo** — ball speeds up over a rally; tension builds.
5. **Shared Screen, Two Minds** — local competition generates body language, eye contact, trash talk.

## Rules

### Setup

- Two vertical paddles (left + right).
- One ball.
- Dashed vertical line divides the court.
- Score: two digits per player, top of screen.

### Round

- Ball serves from center, random direction (up-left or down-right, etc.).
- Each player moves their paddle vertically to intercept.
- On paddle-hit: ball reflects; angle depends on where on the paddle it strikes.
- On wall-hit (top/bottom): ball reflects.
- On miss (ball crosses end-wall): opponent scores; ball re-serves.

### Match

- First to 11 points wins.
- Some variants: 15, 21.
- No time limit; match ends at target score.

## Gameplay Mechanics

### Paddle

- Moves vertically only.
- Paddle speed is fixed per player control (dial on original cabinet).
- Paddle cannot leave screen bounds (clamped).
- 8-zone hit spots (original): ball angle depends on which zone of the paddle was hit.

### Ball

- Constant velocity between hits.
- Speed increases after certain number of paddle hits (4, 12, 16 in original).
- Reflection: mirror vertical component on wall; reverse horizontal on paddle hit.
- Angle adjustment: top/bottom of paddle produces steep angle; center produces flat.

### Serving

- After a score, ball serves from center after a brief pause.
- Direction toward the player who did NOT score (or random, depending on version).
- Original: serve direction alternates.

### Scoring

- Point when ball crosses opponent's end-wall.
- Score displayed top-center, one number per player (00-15 typical range).

## Input

### Original Arcade Cabinet

- Per-player rotary dial (potentiometer) for paddle position.
- Coin slot.
- No buttons.

## AI (Single-Player Variants)

Many later Pong variants added a CPU opponent:
- **Tracking AI**: move paddle toward ball Y position (simple).
- **Predictive AI**: compute where ball will arrive at opposing paddle.
- **Tunable difficulty**: introduce small tracking error + reaction delay.

## Audio

Three sounds total on original hardware:
- **Paddle hit**: high blip.
- **Wall hit**: low blip.
- **Score**: longer buzz.

Generated by simple square-wave oscillators triggered by hit events.

## Visual Design

- Black background.
- White paddles (4-pixel-wide rectangles originally).
- White square ball.
- Center court dashed line (column of dashes).
- Pixelated digits for score.

No textures, no gradients, no color — pure monochrome. Visual economy as feature.

## Original Hardware

Pong had no CPU. Logic implemented directly via:
- TTL counters generating horizontal + vertical beam positions.
- Comparators for ball position vs. paddle position (collision).
- Flip-flops for scoring state.
- NAND gates for reflection logic.
- 555 timers for sound generation.

Cabinet hardware ran at 60 Hz directly driving NTSC signal. No framebuffer; screen drawn scanline-by-scanline from raw logic.

The game's "code" was a schematic, reproducible on a breadboard.

## Sequels / Variants

- **Pong Doubles** (1973): 2v2.
- **Super Pong** (1974): 4-player.
- **Pong Home Console** (1975): first home version — Atari's home Pong.
- **Video Olympics** (Atari 2600): 50+ variants.
- Countless homebrew, clones, classroom recreations.

## Cultural Impact

- Pong is the Rosetta Stone of video games: every "first game you code" is a Pong variant.
- Introduced the concept of shared-screen local multiplayer.
- Seeded Atari → Nolan Bushnell's empire → industry.
- "Ball bouncing between paddles" is the fundamental primitive that scaled to Breakout, Arkanoid, Air Hockey, Rocket League, etc.

## Design Philosophy

### Minimalism as Depth

The rule set fits on a napkin. The tactical depth is infinite — angle management, pace control, pre-reading, psychological warfare.

### Physical Feel First

Pong's magic is in paddle-ball collision physics. Too-simple reflection is boring; proper angle-based reflection creates expressive rallies.

### Zero Simulation Layer

Pong has no AI, no economy, no progression, no procedural generation. All complexity comes from two humans battling each other.

### Audio as Primary Feedback

The blip is essential — it signals the hit moment and pulls the player into the rhythm. Silent Pong is worse Pong.

### Coin-Op Cadence

Match length (1-5 minutes) tuned for arcade revenue model: long enough to satisfy, short enough to free the cabinet for the next quarter.

## Reconstruction Complexity

Implementing Pong today:
- **Beginner**: ~100 lines of JavaScript + HTML5 Canvas.
- **Intermediate**: Unity/Godot/GameMaker template.
- **Advanced**: FPGA re-implementation of original TTL schematics.

Pong is the canonical "hello world" of game dev. Every engine's first tutorial.
